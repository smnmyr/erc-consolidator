  ---
  Scaling Solid Without Hyperfinancialization

  The Core Risk to Name Clearly

  The danger isn't Inrupt per se — it's the platform trap: an open protocol where one commercial actor accumulates enough network
  effects, enterprise contracts, and tooling dominance that the "open" spec becomes nominal while real control is private. This is
  how XMPP died as a consumer protocol (Google Talk, then abandonment), and how LinkedIn captures "professional identity" while
  nominally being a website.

  ---
  What Actually Works: Lessons From Protocols That Stayed Open

  Email is the canonical success. SMTP/IMAP scales globally, nobody got hyperrich from the protocol itself, and it remains
  federated. It scaled through: ISPs with existing infrastructure, organizations self-hosting, and a boring-but-real standards
  process. The lesson: utility infrastructure doesn't need a VC growth story.

  ActivityPub/Mastodon is a more recent example — imperfect, but it demonstrates that federated social infrastructure can exist
  without financialization if the hosting economics are manageable.

  ---
  Specific Mechanisms for Solid

  1. Public sector as the primary scaling engine
  The Advisory Committee already voted to prioritize Health, Public Administration, and Education. This is the right instinct.
  Municipalities, national health services, and universities can run pod infrastructure as public utility — the way they run
  libraries or sewage systems. Belgium's Athumi (formerly Digipolis Vlaanderen) is already doing this at regional scale. Flanders
  essentially bet on Solid as civic infrastructure. That model — state as host, citizen as owner — is the non-financialized path.

  The EU context is favorable: GDPR, the Data Act, and data spaces initiatives (Gaia-X, iSHARE) create regulatory pull for exactly
  this kind of sovereign data infrastructure.

  2. Data cooperatives for consumer hosting
  MIDATA (Switzerland) and similar cooperatives offer pod-like hosting under member governance. Users are members, not customers;
  surplus goes back to members, not shareholders. This is the mutual/cooperative answer to "who pays for hosting?" The MyData
  operator model that Viivi Lähteenoja's movement developed — certified operators accountable to users rather than investors —
  could be formalized as a Solid pod operator standard.

  3. Keep the Community Solid Server (CSS) healthy
  Ruben Verborgh's team at imec/KU Leuven built the CSS as a genuinely open, modular reference implementation. This is
  strategically critical: it prevents Inrupt's Enterprise Solid Server from becoming the de facto standard through tooling lock-in
  even if the spec remains open. The Advisory Committee's decision to not invest €3M in an enterprise server and instead offer free
   licenses was correct — it keeps the ecosystem from consolidating around one commercial implementation.

  4. Resist ACP winning by default
  The WAC/ACP dispute has a financialization dimension that's rarely stated explicitly: ACP is more expressive and
  enterprise-friendly, which advantages Inrupt's commercial products (more complex access control = more need for sophisticated
  tooling = more need for Inrupt). WAC is simpler, more accessible to community implementations, and less of a moat. Resolving this
   in favor of genuine interoperability — rather than whichever system Inrupt implements best — matters for keeping the ecosystem
  open.

  5. Grant funding for core infrastructure, not just research
  NLnet Foundation, EU Horizon, national research councils, and civic tech funders already support Solid-adjacent work. The gap is
  that grants fund research but rarely fund the unglamorous ongoing maintenance of shared infrastructure (the solidcommunity.net
  problem the minutes describe). Dedicated infrastructure grants — modeled on how the Linux Foundation or Apache Software
  Foundation operates — would reduce the dependency on Inrupt to fill the gap by default.

  6. Separate the stack: commons vs. services
  The protocol, pod server software, and core specs are commons — they should be publicly funded and governed. Value-added services
   (apps, analytics, enterprise integrations, convenience features) can be commercial. The problem occurs when a commercial actor
  controls both layers. The ODI stewardship transition is an attempt to structurally separate them; it needs to be defended and
  deepened.

  ---
  The Hard Truth

  Solid has a "last mile" problem that email didn't: email piggybacked on organizational server infrastructure that already
  existed. Most people don't run their own mail servers — ISPs and employers do it for them, for unrelated reasons. Solid needs an
  equivalent: an institution that has other reasons to maintain the infrastructure (governments serving citizens, universities
  serving students, health systems serving patients) so that pod hosting doesn't require a VC-funded business model to be
  economically sustainable.

  The public sector route is the most promising non-financialized scaling path — but it requires Solid to demonstrate value to
  procurement officers, not just developers. That's a different kind of work than the community has historically done, and it's
  probably where the tension between the grassroots contributors and the institutional actors (ODI, Inrupt) is most acute right
  now.
